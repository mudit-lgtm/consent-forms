What to Include

  • Interviewer/organization name and contact
  • Type of recording: audio, video, or both
  • Purpose of the recording
  • How and where the recording will be stored
  • Who has access to the recording
  • How the recording will be used (transcription, publication, broadcast, archiving)
  • Retention period and deletion policy
  • Option to refuse recording while still participating verbally
  • Right to request recording stopped during interview
  • Right to request deletion of recording after interview
  • Separate consent options for audio and video
  • Signature and date lines

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need consent before recording an interview? ▼
In most jurisdictions, yes — especially if you plan to use or publish the recording. Many states and countries have 'two-party consent' or 'all-party consent' laws requiring everyone in a conversation to consent to being recorded, even in private settings.
What is a two-party consent law? ▼
Two-party (or all-party) consent laws require all parties in a conversation to consent to being recorded. These laws exist in many US states (including California, Florida, Illinois) and various other countries. Always check local laws before recording.
Can a participant consent to audio but not video? ▼
Yes. Your consent form should offer separate checkboxes for audio and video consent, allowing participants to choose their comfort level with each type of recording.
What should I do if a participant wants to stop the recording? ▼
Always honor a participant's request to stop recording immediately. Pause or stop the device, confirm the recording has stopped, and continue the interview on a notes-only basis if the participant wishes to continue.
How long should I keep interview recordings? ▼
Retention periods vary by institution and research type. Academic research typically requires 3–7 years. For journalism, recordings may need to be retained indefinitely as source material. For commercial research, follow your organization's data retention policy.
