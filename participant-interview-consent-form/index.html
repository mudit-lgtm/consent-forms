What Is a Participant Interview Consent Form?

A participant interview consent form is a legal document that must be signed by a research participant before any interview takes place. It serves a dual purpose: it ensures participants fully understand what they are agreeing to, and it provides researchers with documented proof that informed, voluntary consent was obtained.

Under U.S. federal regulations — specifically 45 CFR 46, commonly known as the Common Rule — any research involving human subjects requires written informed consent unless a specific waiver applies. The consent form is the primary mechanism for satisfying this requirement.

Unlike a general release form or a simple waiver, a participant interview consent form must cover several specific legal and ethical elements including the nature of the study, confidentiality protections, risks and benefits, and — critically — the participant's absolute right to withdraw at any time without penalty.

The Belmont Report & Participant Rights

The Belmont Report (1979), published by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, is the foundational ethical framework for research involving human subjects in the United States. Every IRB-reviewed study — and every participant consent form — must align with its three core principles.

Three Belmont Report Principles Every Consent Form Must Reflect

Published by the U.S. DHHS National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects (1979). Codified in 45 CFR 46 (the Common Rule).

Principle 1

Respect for Persons

Individuals must be treated as autonomous agents. Participation must be voluntary, with full information provided and withdrawal permitted at any time.

Principle 2

Beneficence

Researchers must maximise potential benefits and minimise possible harms. The consent form must clearly state any foreseeable risks and how they will be mitigated.

Principle 3

Justice

The benefits and burdens of research must be distributed fairly. Vulnerable populations must not be exploited. Participant selection criteria must be disclosed.

In practice, these three principles translate directly into the required content of your consent form. The Respect for Persons principle mandates the voluntary participation clause and the right-to-withdraw statement. Beneficence requires a clear risks-and-benefits section. Justice means you must explain who is eligible to participate and why.

8 Required Elements Under 45 CFR 46

The Common Rule (45 CFR 46.116) specifies exactly what a valid informed consent form must contain. Missing even one of these elements can result in IRB rejection or, in a worst case, invalidate your research data entirely.

✅ IRB Required Elements Checklist

  • Study purpose and expected duration — A clear, plain-language explanation of why the research is being conducted and approximately how long the interview will take
  • Description of procedures — What will happen during the interview: will it be recorded? Will notes be taken? Will a transcript be shared?
  • Foreseeable risks or discomforts — Any potential psychological, social, or professional risks from participation, even if minimal
  • Reasonably expected benefits — Direct benefits to the participant (if any) and broader benefits to society or the field
  • Confidentiality protections — How data will be stored, who will have access, whether quotes will be attributed, and when data will be destroyed
  • Voluntary participation statement — Explicit language that participation is not required, and refusal will have no negative consequences
  • Right to withdraw without penalty — Participants may stop at any point during the interview without any consequence to their relationship with the institution
  • Contact information — Researcher contact details AND a separate IRB or ethics board contact for questions about participant rights

The 2018 update to the Common Rule (effective January 21, 2019) added a requirement for a concise summary at the beginning of the consent form — often called the "key information" section — that highlights the most important aspects before the participant reads the full document. This is now a standard element in modern IRB-compliant consent forms.

Voluntary Participation — What It Actually Means

The phrase "participation is voluntary" is the single most important statement in any research consent form. Yet it is frequently misunderstood. Voluntary participation means more than just saying "you can say no" — it encompasses a cluster of rights that must all be clearly communicated.

  1. The right to decline entirely — A participant can refuse to take part before the interview begins with no explanation required
  2. The right to skip questions — During the interview, participants may decline to answer any individual question without withdrawing from the study
  3. The right to withdraw mid-interview — A participant can stop the interview at any point and their data will be excluded from the study (unless they agree otherwise)
  4. The right to withdraw after the interview — In most jurisdictions, participants may withdraw their data for a specified period (typically 2–4 weeks) after the interview concludes
  5. No penalty or coercion — There must be no implicit or explicit suggestion that declining will affect grades, employment, access to services, or any other outcome

Courts and IRBs have repeatedly found that consent obtained under coercive circumstances — even subtle ones, such as a professor asking students to participate in their own study — may not constitute valid voluntary consent. If there is any power differential between researcher and participant, additional safeguards must be documented in the consent form.

Participant Consent vs. Informed Consent: Key Differences

Researchers frequently use these two terms interchangeably, but they carry distinct meanings in formal ethics contexts. Understanding the difference matters when preparing IRB submissions.

Feature Participant Consent Form Informed Consent Form
Primary focus Agreement to participate Full disclosure + agreement
Information depth Study overview sufficient Comprehensive disclosure required
Risk/benefit disclosure Sometimes Always required
IRB "key information" section Recommended Required (2018 Common Rule)
Suitable for minimal-risk studies Yes Yes
Suitable for sensitive topics Not recommended Yes — required

Used in interview research Yes Yes

For most interview-based research — academic dissertations, qualitative studies, journalism, UX research — the distinction is academic. What matters practically is that the consent form you use covers all eight required elements listed above and is approved by your institution's ethics body before data collection begins.

How to Use This Consent Form: 5 Steps

Using a participant interview consent form correctly involves more than just getting a signature. Follow these five steps to ensure your consent process is both ethically sound and practically defensible.

1

Download and customise the template

Download the Word (.docx) version and fill in your specific study title, institution name, IRB protocol number, researcher contact details, and IRB/ethics committee contact information. Every blank field must be completed before the form is shown to participants.

2

Submit to your IRB or ethics committee for approval

Before approaching any participant, your consent form must be reviewed and approved by your Institutional Review Board (IRB) or equivalent ethics committee. Most IRBs require submission of the consent form as part of the full protocol application. Allow 2–6 weeks for standard review.

3

Share with participants at least 24–48 hours in advance

Send the completed, approved consent form to participants before the interview date. This gives them adequate time to read the document, consider their participation, and raise any questions. Pressuring someone to sign immediately before an interview undermines the voluntariness of consent.

4

Review key points verbally at the start of the interview

Even if the participant has read the form in advance, briefly walk through the most important points at the beginning of the interview: the study purpose, the voluntary nature of participation, confidentiality protections, and the right to withdraw. For remote interviews, this verbal review should be recorded or documented.

5

Obtain signature and store records securely

Collect a signed copy before the interview begins. For in-person interviews, a handwritten signature is standard. For remote interviews, a digital signature (DocuSign, Adobe Sign) or an emailed confirmation of consent is acceptable in most jurisdictions. Retain the signed form for a minimum of 3 years (7 years for federally funded research).

Confidentiality, Anonymity & Data Protection

One of the most common sources of confusion in research consent forms is the distinction between confidentiality and anonymity. These are not the same thing, and conflating them in your consent form can expose you to serious ethical and legal liability.

  • Anonymity means the researcher does not know and cannot discover the identity of the participant. This is rare in interview research, since the researcher is speaking directly with the participant.
  • Confidentiality means the researcher knows who the participant is but commits to protecting that identity — for example, by using pseudonyms in publications, restricting access to raw data, and securely destroying recordings after transcription.

Your consent form must specify exactly which level of protection you are offering. If you are using direct quotes in your research output, state whether quotes will be attributed or anonymised. If audio recordings will be made, specify how long they will be retained and when they will be deleted. Vague confidentiality statements are a common reason for IRB revision requests.

Under GDPR (if your participants are located in the EU or UK), consent must be specific, informed, unambiguous, and freely given. You must also disclose the lawful basis for processing personal data under Article 6 GDPR. For most academic research, this will be "legitimate interests" or "scientific research" under Article 89. If your study involves participants outside the USA, consider adding a separate GDPR-compliant addendum to your consent form.

Remote and Online Interviews — Special Considerations

Video and phone interviews have become the dominant format for qualitative research since 2020. Remote interviews introduce specific consent challenges that in-person interviews do not.

  • Recording disclosure is essential: If you are recording the video call (via Zoom, Teams, or similar), this must be explicitly stated in the consent form and verbally confirmed at the start of the call. In two-party consent states (California, Florida, Illinois, Pennsylvania, and others), recording without consent from all parties is illegal.
  • Screenshot and transcript consent: If you intend to capture screenshots or share transcripts, this must be separately disclosed. Many participants consent to audio recording but not to video screenshots.
  • Digital signature acceptability: Most IRBs now accept electronic consent for remote research. Acceptable formats include: emailed reply confirming consent, digital signature via DocuSign/Adobe Sign, or a REDCap-based e-consent form. Check with your IRB before using electronic consent.
  • Platform security: Your consent form should name the platform being used (e.g., Zoom, Microsoft Teams) and note that you cannot guarantee the security of third-party platforms.
