How to Write a Consent Form for an Interview

What Is the Purpose of an Interview Consent Form?

The purpose of an interview consent form is to ensure that participants give truly informed agreement before an interview begins. It documents that the participant understands what they are agreeing to — including how their data will be used — and that they are participating voluntarily. Consent forms protect both the participant (by preserving their rights) and the researcher or interviewer (by documenting ethical compliance).

Step 1: Identify the Study Purpose

Begin with clear identification of the interview project at the top of the form:

  • Full title of the study, project, podcast, or article
  • Researcher/interviewer name and institutional affiliation
  • IRB protocol number (if applicable)
  • Contact email and phone number
  • Date of the consent form

Tip: Write at a 6th-8th grade reading level. Use plain language. Avoid academic jargon and passive voice. The participant must be able to read and understand the form before signing.

Step 2: Describe Participant Involvement

Explain clearly what the participant will be asked to do:

  • Format of the interview (in-person, phone, video, email, etc.)
  • Expected duration of the interview
  • General topics or subject areas to be discussed
  • Whether the interview will be audio/video recorded
  • Whether the participant will be asked to review their transcript

Step 3: State Risks and Benefits

Even if the risks of your interview are minimal, you must address them. Most interview research carries no more than everyday risk (e.g., time commitment, mild discomfort from sensitive questions). Benefits may include contributing to research knowledge, professional recognition, or self-reflection.

Example risk statement: "Participation involves minimal risk. Some interview questions relate to [topic area]; you are free to skip any question."

Step 4: Explain Confidentiality

The confidentiality section is often the most important to participants. Be specific:

  • State whether responses will be anonymized or attributed by name
  • Describe how recordings and transcripts will be stored (encrypted, password-protected)
  • Name who will have access to the raw data
  • State how long data will be retained and when/how it will be destroyed
  • Note any limits to confidentiality (e.g., mandatory reporting for harm-to-self)

Step 5: Include Voluntary Participation Statement

This is a non-negotiable element. The consent form must explicitly state that:

  • Participation is entirely voluntary
  • Participants may decline to answer any question
  • Participants may withdraw at any time without penalty
  • Withdrawal will have no negative consequences for the participant
  • For employee research: decisions will not affect employment status

Step 6: Add Signature Block

Close the form with a clear consent statement and signature fields:

  • Participant name (print)
  • Participant signature
  • Date signed
  • Researcher/interviewer signature
  • Date signed by researcher
  • For minors: parent/guardian name, signature, and relationship to participant

Checklist: What Every Consent Form Must Include

  • Study/project title and researcher identification
  • IRB/ethics protocol number (if required)
  • Purpose statement in plain language
  • Procedure description (format, duration, topics)
  • Recording consent (if applicable)
  • Risks and benefits statement
  • Confidentiality provisions and data storage
  • Voluntary participation and withdrawal rights
  • Researcher and IRB contact information
  • Participant and researcher signature block
  • Parent/guardian signature block (if participant is a minor)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Consent forms written in dense academic or legal language may not be understood by participants, making the consent invalid. Write at a 6th-8th grade reading level and use short sentences.

This is legally and ethically required. Every consent form must state clearly that participation is voluntary and withdrawal is penalty-free.

Even if a participant consents to the interview, they may not consent to recording. Always obtain separate, explicit consent for audio and/or video recording.

For formal research, the consent form must include the IRB or ethics committee contact information so participants can ask about their rights independently.

The consent form must be signed before the interview begins — not after. Retroactive consent is not valid.
