Qualitative Interview Consent Form

Free consent form templates for qualitative research interviews — semi-structured, ethnographic, in-depth, narrative, and life history interviews. IRB-compliant.

What Makes Qualitative Interview Consent Forms Unique

Qualitative research interviews are exploratory in nature, often covering sensitive personal experiences, cultural practices, or lived histories. Unlike structured surveys, qualitative interviews are flexible and open-ended — participants may share more than anticipated. This makes robust informed consent especially critical.

Key considerations unique to qualitative interview consent:

  • Participants may share unexpectedly sensitive information — consent must address unforeseen disclosures
  • Transcripts may be identifiable even when pseudonyms are used — address de-identification limits
  • Long-term ethnographic studies may require ongoing consent, not just one-time signing
  • Participants may wish to review and correct their transcripts — include this option if offered

Consent for Semi-Structured Interviews

Semi-structured interviews follow a general guide but allow for flexible follow-up questions based on participant responses. The consent form should describe the general topic areas while noting that the exact questions may vary. Download the general template → and adapt it for semi-structured use.

Ethnographic Interview Consent Form

Ethnographic research often involves extended engagement with communities over time. Consent forms for ethnographic interviews may need to address:

  • Community-level consent in addition to individual consent
  • Ongoing consent for extended fieldwork periods
  • Permissions around photographs, field notes, and audio recordings
  • Data sovereignty for indigenous or marginalized communities
  • Return of findings and member checking processes

In-Depth Interview Consent Form

QUALITATIVE RESEARCH INTERVIEW — INFORMED CONSENT Study Title: [Study Title] Researcher: [Name], [Institution], [Dept/Program] Type of Interview: ☐ Semi-structured ☐ Ethnographic ☐ In-depth ☐ Life history IRB Protocol: [#] Ethics Approval: [Reference] Contact: [Email] | [Phone] PURPOSE AND METHODS This qualitative study explores [topic] through in-depth interviews with [participant group]. The interviews are open-ended and exploratory in nature — there are no right or wrong answers. Your perspectives and experiences are the focus. INTERVIEW DETAILS • Duration: approximately [X] minutes/hours • Format: [in person / by phone / via video call] • Topics: [brief description of themes/areas] • The interview will be audio recorded with your permission • You may review the transcript of your interview if desired CONFIDENTIALITY AND ANONYMIZATION Your identity will be protected. A pseudonym will be assigned to replace your name in all published materials. While we take extensive measures to protect anonymity, please be aware that in qualitative research, some details may remain identifiable — particularly in small communities. VOLUNTARY PARTICIPATION Participation is voluntary. You may stop the interview at any time, decline to answer any question, or request the withdrawal of your data up to [date] after the interview. CONSENT I have been fully informed about this qualitative research study and voluntarily agree to participate. ☐ I consent to the interview being audio recorded. ☐ I would like the opportunity to review my transcript. Participant Signature: ___________________________ Date: ____________ Researcher Signature: ____________________________ Date: ____________

Key Informant Interview Consent Form

Key informant interviews involve experts, community leaders, or stakeholders with specialized knowledge. Because participants are often identifiable by their role or position, consent forms must carefully address whether participants will be identified by name or title, and obtain explicit consent for attribution.

Life History Interview Consent Form

Life history and oral history interviews capture personal narratives over a lifetime. These interviews often result in archivable recordings that may be stored and accessed in the future. Consent forms for life history interviews should address long-term archiving, future use, and whether consent is required again if the archive is accessed years later.

Sociology Interview Consent Form

Sociology research often involves interviews with vulnerable populations, communities affected by structural inequalities, or participants discussing sensitive social topics. Following full informed consent standards → is essential. The American Sociological Association (ASA) Code of Ethics requires informed consent for all research involving human subjects.
