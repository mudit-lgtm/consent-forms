What to Include

  • Publication name, media outlet, and journalist name
  • Topic of the interview
  • How quotes will be attributed (full name, pseudonym, background, anonymous)
  • Recording consent: audio and/or video
  • Right of interviewee to go off the record
  • Whether the interviewee can review quotes before publication
  • Broadcast/publication rights for print, online, and social media
  • Contact information for the journalist and editor
  • Date of interview
  • Signature line for interviewee and journalist

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Do journalists need interview consent forms? ▼
While not always legally required, using a journalism interview consent form is considered best practice. It documents what was agreed, protects against disputes about attribution, and demonstrates professional standards. In some jurisdictions and for certain sensitive interviews, written consent may be legally required.
What is an on-the-record interview consent form? ▼
An on-the-record consent form documents that an interviewee has agreed to be quoted by name in a published article or broadcast. This creates a clear record protecting both the journalist and the source.
Can I use consent forms for oral history interviews? ▼
Yes. An oral history interview consent form is important for documenting permission to record, archive, and use historical testimony in research, publications, and archives. Many historical societies and universities require them.
What does 'background' vs 'off the record' mean? ▼
Background means the journalist can use the information but not attribute it directly to the named source. Off the record means the information should not be published at all — only used for context or to guide further research.
Do student journalists need consent forms? ▼
Yes, student journalists should use consent forms for formal interviews, particularly when working on published student newspapers, broadcast segments, or journalism course projects. Institutions like SNHU and ASU often provide guidelines.
