What Is Informed Consent in Interviewing?

Informed consent in interviewing is the ethical and legal process by which a potential participant is provided all relevant information about a study or interview — including its purpose, procedures, risks, and benefits — before being asked to agree to participate. The term "informed" is critical: the participant must genuinely understand what they are consenting to, not just sign a form.

The concept of informed consent originates from medical ethics and the Belmont Report (1979), which established three core principles for research involving human subjects: respect for persons (autonomy), beneficence, and justice. Today, informed consent is required by virtually all IRBs, university ethics committees, and professional research associations.

For interviews specifically, informed consent means the participant understands:

  • The purpose and goals of the interview research
  • What they will be asked to do and for how long
  • Whether the interview will be recorded and how recordings will be used
  • How their data will be stored, protected, and potentially published
  • Whether they can remain anonymous or will be identified
  • That participation is voluntary and they can withdraw at any time

Required Elements of an Informed Consent Form

The U.S. federal regulations (45 CFR 46) and most international ethics frameworks require the following elements in an informed consent form for interviews:

  • Study identification — Full title of the study, institution, and IRB/ethics protocol number
  • Purpose statement — Clear, plain-language explanation of what the research is about
  • Procedures — What participants will do, how long it will take, and interview format
  • Recording consent — Explicit consent for audio/video recording if applicable
  • Risks — Any foreseeable physical, psychological, social, or legal risks (even if minimal)
  • Benefits — Any direct benefits to participants or societal benefits of the research
  • Confidentiality — How data will be protected, anonymized, and stored
  • Voluntary participation — Explicit statement that participation is voluntary and withdrawal is penalty-free
  • Contact information — Researcher and IRB/ethics board contact details
  • Signature block — Participant and researcher name, signature, and date

Informed Consent Form for Qualitative Interview

Qualitative research — including semi-structured interviews, ethnographic interviews, focus groups, and life history interviews — requires particularly careful attention to informed consent, since participants often share personal or sensitive information. The study aims to [describe research objectives]. Findings will contribute to [academic field/publication/thesis]. INTERVIEW PROCEDURES You are being invited to participate in one [semi-structured / in-depth / narrative] interview lasting approximately [X] minutes/hours. The interview will take place [location/format: in person, by phone, via Zoom]. You will be asked to share your experiences and perspectives on [describe topics]. With your consent, the interview will be audio-recorded for transcription purposes. Transcripts will be reviewed for accuracy and may be used in research publications. VOLUNTARY NATURE OF PARTICIPATION Your participation is entirely voluntary. You may decline to answer any question, request to stop the interview at any time, or withdraw your data up to [date/timeframe] after the interview, without any penalty or negative consequences. CONFIDENTIALITY AND DATA PROTECTION All interview data will be kept strictly confidential. Your name will be replaced with a pseudonym in all research materials and publications. Audio recordings will be stored on encrypted, password-protected devices and deleted after transcription is complete. Anonymized transcripts will be retained for [X years] for research verification purposes and accessible only to the research team. POTENTIAL RISKS The risks of participation are minimal and similar to those of everyday conversation. If any question causes discomfort, you may skip it without explanation. POTENTIAL BENEFITS There is no direct personal benefit from participation. Your contribution may help advance knowledge about [topic] and benefit [describe broader community or field]. Informed Consent Form for Interview Research

For formal research studies — especially those requiring IRB approval — your informed consent form must strictly follow federal or institutional guidelines. Key differences from a basic consent form include the requirement to list foreseeable risks, state any compensation, and provide IRB contact information.

IRB Tip: Many institutions require consent forms to be written at a 6th–8th grade reading level. Avoid legal jargon and use short sentences. Your IRB may review and request revisions to your consent form before approving your research. See IRB consent form requirements →

How to Write an Informed Consent Form for Interviewing

1

Start with Study Identification

Include the study title, your name and institution, supervisor name, and IRB protocol number at the top of the form.

2

Write a Plain-Language Purpose Statement

Describe what the study is about in simple language. Avoid academic jargon. The participant should immediately understand what they're being asked to contribute to.

3

Describe Interview Procedures in Detail

Specify the interview format, expected duration, topics to be discussed, and whether recording will occur. Clarity here builds trust and reduces anxiety.

4

Address All Required Ethical Elements

Cover risks, benefits, confidentiality, voluntary participation, and withdrawal rights. Even if risks are minimal, they must be acknowledged.

5

Add Signature and Consent Statement

Close with a clear statement of consent and signature fields for both participant and researcher. Consider adding checkboxes for specific consents (e.g., recording).
